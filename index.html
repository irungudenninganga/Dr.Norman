Dr. Norman Borlaug

The man who saved a billion lives

DR Norman Borloug, third from left, trains biologist in mexico on how to increase wheat yields-part of his life war on hunger.

Here's a time line of DR.Norma Borlaug's life :

  • 1914-Born in creso, lowa
  • 1933 -Leaves his family's farm to attend the university of Minnesto,thanks to a Depression era program known as the 'National youth Administration'
  • 1935 -Has to stop school and save up more money. Works in the civilian conversation corps, helping starving Americans "I saw how food changed them", he said , "All of his left scars on me."
  • 1937 -Finishes university and takes a job in the US forestry service
  • 1938-Marries wife of 69 years Margret Gibson. Get laid off to budget cuts. Inspired by Elvin Charles Stakman, he returns to school study under stakman, who teaches him about breeding pest-resistent plants.
  • 1941-Tries to enroll in the millitary after the pearl Harbox attack, but is rejected . Instead , the millitary asked his labs to work on waterproof glue, DDT to control malaria, disenfectants, and other applied science.
  • 1942-Recieves a PHD in Genetic and plant pathology
  • 1944-Rejects a 100% salary increase from Dupont leaves behind his pregnant wife, and flies to Mexico to head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16 years, his team breed 6.000 diffrent strains of disease resistent what - including diffrent varieties of each major climate on Earth.
  • 1945-Discovers a way to grown wheat twice each season, doubling wheat yields
  • 1953-Croses a short, study dwarf breed of wheet with high-yeilding American breed,creating a strain that responds well to fertalizer. It goes on to provide 95% of mexico's wheat
  • 1962-Visits Delhi and brings his high-yielding strains of wheat to the indian subcontinent in time to help mitigate mass starvation due to rapidly expanding population
  • 1970-Recieves the Nobel Peace Prize
  • 1983_Helps seven African countries dramatically increase their maize and sorghum population
  • 1984-Becomes distinguished proffesor at Texas A&M university
  • 2005-States we will have a double the word food supply by 2050. Argues that genetically modified crops are the only way we can meet the demand, as we run out of arable land says that GM crops are not inherently dangerous because we've been genetically modifying plants and animals for a long time. Long before we called it science, people wee selecting the best breeds.
  • 2009-Dies at the age of 95
